Summary

Autonomous vehicles depend heavily on accurate traffic light recognition to ensure safe navigation. However, vision-based systems are vulnerable to adversarial perturbations and environmental lighting variations, which may lead to critical misclassifications. This thesis investigates the adversarial impact of both digital and physical lighting conditions on traffic light recognition models. In the digital attack scenario, adversarial noise was generated using the FGSM and PGD techniques to evaluate whether a VGG16-based classification model could be misled. For the physical attack, a Unity-based simulation environment was developed, featuring a road intersection with a traffic system. A YOLOv8 model was employed for traffic light detection, while the VGG16 classifier processed the detected regions. Various lighting conditions, such as car headlights at different intensities, building lights, and day or night variations, were introduced to induce misclassification. Experimental results show that both digital perturbations and environmental lighting can significantly reduce classification accuracy; green, red, and yellow lights are misclassified under certain illumination conditions. These findings demonstrate that environmental lighting can serve as a form of physical adversarial attack, posing risks to autonomous driving systems. This research highlights the vulnerability of autonomous systems to real-world lighting conditions.

Methodology

Digital Attack

Digital attacks were performed on the VGG16 classification model. FGSM (Fast Gradient Sign Method) was applied in both one-step and minimal-step variations to test model robustness. PGD (Projected Gradient Descent) attacks were also used to evaluate adversarial vulnerability.

Physical Attack

Physical attacks were conducted in a Unity 3D simulation environment representing a road intersection. The workflow involved communication between Unity and an API to control lighting conditions and trigger attacks. Headlights, building lights, and day/night variations were applied to induce misclassification. The YOLOv8 model detected traffic lights, while the VGG16 classifier processed the detected regions.
